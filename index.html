Are Total Points Scored the best metric?

The general trend observed in the scatter plot, where an increase in total points is associated with an increase in win shares, suggests a positive correlation between scoring more points and contributing more to team success (as measured by win shares). This correlation implies that players who score more points tend to have a higher impact on their team's performance and are often more valuable players. However, it's important to be cautious about generalizing this correlation to conclude that scoring more points automatically makes a player better. Here are a few reasons why:

  1. Contextual Factors: The correlation between total points and win shares may be influenced by various contextual factors, such as team strategy, playing time, role within the team, and quality of teammates. A player on a weaker team may have more scoring opportunities but fewer win shares compared to a player on a stronger team.
  2. Style of Play: Scoring points is just one aspect of a player's contribution to the game. Other skills like defense, passing, rebounding, leadership, and intangibles also play a significant role in determining a player's overall value. Focusing solely on scoring may overlook these other important aspects.
  3. Efficiency: The efficiency with which points are scored is crucial. A player who scores a high volume of points but does so with poor shooting percentages or at the expense of ball movement and team chemistry may not necessarily contribute positively to overall team success.
  4. Role and Position: Different players have different roles and positions within a team. Scoring may be the primary responsibility of some players (e.g., shooting guards, scoring forwards) while others are expected to focus more on playmaking or defense. Comparing players solely based on their scoring output may overlook their overall contribution in their respective roles.

Are the best players those who can shoot threes well?

The observation that a three-point percentage of about 38-45% resulted in the most win shares suggests a strong correlation between shooting accuracy from beyond the arc and a player's overall impact on the team's success. Here's an explanation of what this finding could mean:

  1. Efficiency and Scoring Impact: A high three-point percentage indicates that a player is making a significant number of their three-point attempts. This level of accuracy suggests that the player is efficient in their scoring, contributing valuable points to their team's offense. By consistently converting three-point shots, these players can create scoring advantages, stretch the defense, and provide valuable spacing for their teammates.
  2. Spacing and Offensive Impact: Shooting at a high percentage from beyond the arc forces opposing defenses to pay close attention to the player, leading to increased defensive focus and potentially opening up opportunities for other players. This spacing can create driving lanes, attract double-teams, and generate open shots for teammates. Consequently, the player's ability to shoot at a high three-point percentage positively impacts their team's offensive performance, leading to increased scoring and, consequently, more win shares.
  3. Strategic Importance: In today's basketball landscape, the three-point shot has gained significant strategic importance. Teams emphasize shooting efficiency and rely on three-pointers as a valuable scoring weapon. Players who excel in shooting from beyond the arc at a high percentage demonstrate their ability to adapt to this strategic emphasis and contribute effectively to their team's offensive game plan. Consequently, their contributions in terms of scoring efficiency translate into higher win shares.

    4. It's important to note that while the observation indicates a strong correlation, it doesn't imply a causal relationship. Other factors, such as defensive contributions, playmaking abilities, leadership, and overall team dynamics, also influence win shares. Nevertheless, the finding highlights the significance of shooting accuracy from beyond the arc and its impact on a player's individual and team success.
